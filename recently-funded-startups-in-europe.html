Recently funded startups in Europe: where to find them and how to sell

RoundSignal · Updated July 2026 · 8 min read

The short answer: European rounds are announced on Tech.eu, EU-Startups and Sifted at the pan-European level, and on a dozen national outlets below that — Gründerszene in Germany, Maddyness in France, Silicon Canals in the Benelux, Startupitalia in Italy, and their equivalents in Spain, Poland and the Nordics. That fragmentation is the whole story. It makes Europe annoying to monitor, which is exactly why it stays under-sold: most funded-startup lists cover North America and the UK and stop there. Rounds are smaller, so your price has to be smaller — but access is dramatically better, and the inbox you're writing into isn't already full.

Why Europe is the under-worked half of the market

There is no European TechCrunch. In the US, one publication plus Crunchbase News covers the overwhelming majority of announced rounds, which is why every scraper, list and "funded startups this week" product is built on that spine. Europe has no equivalent single spine: a €3M seed in Munich, a €700k pre-seed in Tallinn and a £6M Series A in London are announced in three different places, in three different editorial styles, and two of them will never appear in an English-language aggregator at all.

For a monitoring tool that's a cost problem. For a salesperson it's an opportunity: the same company that would be receiving forty cold emails a week if it were in San Francisco receives approximately none in Ghent or Turin. Several of the best-known paid lead lists in this space state plainly that they cover North America and the UK — we compare one of them in detail on our Fundraise Insider alternative page. Continental Europe is the coverage gap, and coverage gaps are where reply rates live.

Where the rounds are actually announced

Build the list in two layers.

Layer one — pan-European. These catch most rounds above roughly €1M:

  • Tech.eu — the closest thing to a continental record of funding, with round size and investors stated cleanly.
  • EU-Startups — strong on smaller seed and pre-seed rounds that Tech.eu skips, and unusually good at naming the founders.
  • Sifted — more analysis than announcement, best for context on who's investing in what and for UK and Nordic coverage.

Layer two — national. This is where the small rounds live, and small rounds are often the most reachable buyers:

  • Germany — Gründerszene, Deutsche Startups. Deep coverage of Berlin and Munich, mostly in German.
  • France — Maddyness. France announces a lot of small rounds that never cross into English media.
  • Benelux — Silicon Canals, plus Dealroom-sourced write-ups.
  • Italy, Spain, Portugal — Startupitalia and national tech press; near-zero English competition for attention here.
  • Nordics and Baltics — strong local ecosystems, high English fluency, and founders who reply.

Machine-translate the national sources rather than skipping them — a round announcement is three facts (who, how much, from whom) and translation handles that fine. The general mechanics of assembling, de-duping and date-filtering a list like this are in how to find recently funded startups.

Smaller rounds change your price, not your plan

European rounds at the same nominal stage tend to be smaller than US ones. A European seed that would be labelled pre-seed money in the Bay Area is completely normal, and Series A here often looks like a large US seed. This matters for exactly one thing: the size of the deal you can propose.

Keep the same sanity check we use everywhere — your annual price should land around 0.5–5% of what they raised. On a €2M seed that's a €10k–€50k engagement: a branding sprint, one senior placement, a fractional CTO retainer, a quarter of SDR work. Pitch a €150k annual contract into that round and you're not expensive, you're irrelevant — you've asked for 7.5% of the company's entire runway. Four things that are genuinely different about selling into Europe

  • The calendar is real. August is dead across most of the continent, and so is the stretch from about 20 December to 7 January. A round announced in late July is best worked in the first half of September — not because the window closed, but because the buyer was on a beach for it.
  • English is fine; the tone isn't. Nearly every funded European startup works in English internally, so write in English. But the high-pressure US opener — three exclamation marks, "quick question", a fake deadline — reads as spam much faster here. Plain, specific and unhurried outperforms.
  • GDPR is not optional. Cold B2B email to a European company generally works on a legitimate-interest basis, which requires that the message is actually relevant to the person's job, that you say where you got their details if asked, and that opting out is one click and honoured immediately. That's a constraint on volume, not on outreach — and it's another reason relevance-scored lists beat scraped ones.
  • Invoicing is a real objection. Being able to invoice in euros, handle VAT and reverse charge, and sign a DPA removes friction that a US-only supplier can't. If you have a European entity, say so in the first email — it's a genuine differentiator to a newly funded EU company that has just started building a vendor stack.

Timing: the window is the same, the clock starts later

The best moment to reach a funded company is roughly two to eight weeks after the announcement — past the congratulations noise, inside the planning window, before vendors are chosen. That logic is unchanged in Europe and laid out in the best time to sell to a startup after funding.

One European wrinkle: announcements often lag the actual close by longer than in the US, especially for smaller rounds where the company waits to bundle the news with a product launch or a hire. If a national outlet reports a round and the company's own blog dated it six weeks earlier, start the clock from the company's date. Their planning is already underway.

Who to contact

At pre-seed and seed, go straight to the founder — European seed teams are small, and the founder is still the buyer, the signer and often the person reading the inbox. From Series A onward, aim at whichever function the round was raised to build: a new VP of Sales or Head of Growth for go-to-market, a Head of Marketing for brand and demand, a CTO or VP Engineering for infrastructure and security. The address itself is usually a 30-second job — see how to find a startup founder's email. And read their open roles before writing: the job they posted last week tells you where the money is going and who will own the budget.
